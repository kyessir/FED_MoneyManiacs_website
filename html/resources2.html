Introduction to Inflation and the Inflation in Digital Age

02 January 2024, 08:30pm


What is Inflation?

Inflation is a rise in prices, which can be translated as the decline of purchasing power over time. The rate at which purchasing power drops can be reflected in the average price increase of a basket of selected goods and services over some period of time. The rise in prices, which is often expressed as a percentage, means that a unit of currency effectively buys less than it did in prior periods. Inflation can be contrasted with deflation, which occurs when prices decline and purchasing power increases.


Introduction to Inflation and the Inflation in Digital Age

The average rate of annual global inflation from 1981 to 2020 was 5.4%. In 2020, the global inflation rate was 1.94%. However, the rate varied significantly across countries, ranging from −2.7% in Qatar to 2360% in Venezuela. Cryptocurrency, particularly Bitcoin, has been touted as a potential solution for citizens of countries battling it. The argument is that the decentralization of cryptocurrency offers an alternative to central bank policies, which often struggle to manage the rates. Cryptocurrencies operate independently of significant banks, meaning their value isn’t directly affected by considerable bank policies or national economic performance. This independence can make cryptocurrencies an attractive option for citizens of countries experiencing it where the value of the national currency is rapidly declining.

Why do we need to know Inflation?

  • It is a general increase in prices, which can indicate an imbalance in the overall economy that policymakers need to address.
  • It can reduce your ability to purchase the same goods and services over time, putting stress on your budget and making tight financial situations even tighter.
  • It can have a negative impact on the overall economy, especially if it leads to higher prices for basic necessities such as food.
  • It can cause people to buy less, cut back on non-necessities like travel, movies or eating out or even cut back on necessities like utilities and food.
  • It can cause a drop in purchasing power because of a rise in prices over a certain period of time, making a single unit of currency buy less than it did previously.
